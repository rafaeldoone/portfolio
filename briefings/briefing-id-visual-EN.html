Take your time with this. There are no right or wrong answers — only honest ones. The more you share about your business, the stronger and more precise your visual identity will be.

01

THE BUSINESS

Question 01
What's your brand name and what does it do?
The way you describe your own business says a lot about your positioning. Speak as if you were explaining it to a friend — no jargon needed.
Question 02
How long has the business been around?
A new brand has different needs than one that already has an established audience. This shapes the tone of the solution.
Question 03
Is this a personal brand or a company?
When the founder is the face of the brand, the identity needs to feel more personal. When it's a company, it needs to stand on its own regardless of who's behind it.
Question 04
Who is your brand for? Describe your ideal client in detail.
Your visual identity speaks before you do. It needs to immediately resonate with the people you want to attract — and turn away those who aren't a fit.
Question 05
Who are your 3 closest competitors? What sets you apart?
To create an identity that stands out, I need to know what's already in your client's field of vision. Without that, I risk creating something that looks like more of the same.
02

PERSONALITY

Question 06
If your brand were a person, what would they be like?
This question unlocks what's hard to express in technical terms. Brand personality drives everything — tone of voice, colors, typography, the weight of shapes.
Question 07
Pick 3 to 5 adjectives that describe how your brand should be perceived.
These act as a filter throughout the creative process. When a color or shape decision comes up, they answer whether it's right or not.
Sophisticated Accessible Bold Minimalist Expressive Technical Human Urban Premium Youthful Trustworthy Creative Classic Innovative
Question 08
Which visual style fits your brand best?
This avoids loose interpretations of words like "modern" or "sophisticated" — they mean very different things to different people.
Question 09
Is there a brand identity you admire? Why?
References speak faster than words. It doesn't have to be in your niche — a clothing brand, a restaurant, a band. What matters is the why.
Question 10
Is there anything you absolutely DON'T want in your identity?
Knowing what to avoid is just as important as knowing what to aim for. Colors, styles, and references you hate are just as valuable as the ones you love.
Question 11
Do you have any color preferences or things you'd rather avoid?
Colors carry emotional and cultural memory. Personal preferences matter, but so does what works for your audience and market context.
Question 12
Does your brand need to work on a light background, dark background, or both?
This defines the required versions of the identity from the start. It avoids rework and surprises at delivery.
Question 13
What feeling do you want to spark in someone who sees your brand?
Strong brands don't just look good — they evoke something. Defining that feeling with precision helps create an identity that goes beyond the visual.
03

APPLICATIONS

Question 14
Where will the identity be used? (select all that apply)
A brand that needs to work on physical packaging has very different requirements than one that lives only on Instagram. This defines the necessary versions from the start.
Social media Website Business card Stationery Packaging Uniform Signage Presentations Other
Question 15
Will you need printed materials?
Printing has specific technical requirements — CMYK, bleed, minimum resolution. Knowing this upfront avoids delivering files that don't work with your printer.
Question 16
Do you have professional photos of your brand or products/services?
A well-applied identity depends on images that are visually consistent. If you don't have them, it's worth planning this alongside the project.
04

HISTORY

Question 17
Have you had a visual identity before? What worked and what didn't?
Understanding the history avoids repeating mistakes and respects what's already been built. If there's something your audience already recognizes, that's important data.
Question 18
Is there anything that needs to stay?
This avoids proposing changes to something you consider non-negotiable. Finding this out after delivery creates unnecessary friction.
The brand name A specific color A symbol or icon A typeface Nothing — open to starting fresh
Question 19
Where do you want your brand to be in 3 years?
A visual identity needs to grow with the business. Knowing the future direction avoids creating something that will feel too small or out of place too soon.
Question 20
Is there something about your story or values that your identity should honor?
This is the most important question in the briefing. The best identities have a layer of truth — something that comes from within, not from trends. That's what turns a pretty brand into a memorable one.
05

INVESTMENT

Question 21
How would you prefer to receive project updates?
This aligns expectations before we start. It avoids the anxious client asking for updates every week — or the one who disappears and only shows up to disapprove.
Question 22
Do you have a specific date or deadline to meet?
Urgency changes planning and may be factored into the proposal. If you don't have one, leave it blank.
Question 23
What's your budget for this project?
Transparency here saves time on both sides. No judgment — every budget range allows for a different scope, and it's better to align this upfront than to arrive at a proposal that doesn't work for either of us.
Question 24
How would you prefer to be contacted?
Simple as it sounds, this avoids reaching out through the wrong channel and leaving you wondering why you haven't heard back.
